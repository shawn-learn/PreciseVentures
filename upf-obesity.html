Ultra‑Processed Foods and Obesity

By Shawn Learn BKin. BSc. CSCS

Ultra‑processed foods (UPFs) are industrial formulations created from processed ingredients and additives. According to the Monteiro NOVA classification, items with five or more ingredients—or those containing preservatives, colorings, flavor enhancers, and other industrial agents—are considered UPFs.

The Epidemiological Link

Large observational studies consistently associate higher UPF intake with increased body mass index (BMI) and chronic disease risk. A BMJ meta‑analysis spanning multiple cohorts found strong dose‑response relationships between UPF consumption and long‑term weight gain.

Experimental Findings

The National Institutes of Health (NIH) conducted a controlled feeding trial lasting two weeks. Participants consuming an ultraprocessed diet ate about 500 more calories per day and gained weight relative to a minimally processed menu. Critics note the brief study duration and small sample size mean results may not fully generalize, but follow‑up research is underway. In addition, although macronutrients were matched, the UPF meals were more calorie dense and contained less fiber, making it difficult to completely separate processing effects from nutrient composition.

Another recent dataset from Pontzer et al., PNAS 2025 analyzed total energy expenditure in 4,213 individuals across 34 countries. The study observed that regions with greater access to UPFs tended to show higher average BMI, underscoring the global scope of the issue.

Is Processing the Primary Cause?

While UPFs are likely a major contributor to overeating and weight gain, they are not the sole factor. Genetics, activity levels, and other dietary patterns play important roles. Current randomized evidence demonstrates short‑term weight changes, and longer trials are needed to isolate the influence of processing itself.

Takeaways

  • Minimizing UPFs can help manage energy intake due to lower calorie density and improved satiety from whole foods.
  • Broader epidemiological research links heavy UPF consumption with obesity and chronic diseases including type 2 diabetes.
  • Future trials will clarify how much processing versus nutrient makeup drives these associations.
